We introduce Projection Learning, a variational framework for runtime learning in which state updates are minimal consistency repairs. The update is a Bregman projection onto an evidence set, or its soft proximal analogue. We prove that projection is characterized by a Pythagorean principle: it is exactly the update that satisfies evidence while not increasing distance to any feasible ideal state. We also show that soft Projection Learning is implicit mirror descent, with ordinary gradient descent recovered as a linearized $\ell_2$ special case. We instantiate the framework as Conservative Projection Memory, a neural associative state whose write direction is the innovation of a key relative to previously protected evidence. This yields an algebraic decomposition into redundant, novel, and contradictory evidence. We prove exact binding and capacity laws, conflict impossibility, no-free-repair lower bounds, optimal residual contraction, equivalence between sequential hard updates and batch projection, ridge competitive bounds, log-determinant information growth, and Bayesian interpretations. The resulting algorithms perform runtime adaptation by forward computation plus closed-form state repair, without inference-time backpropagation.

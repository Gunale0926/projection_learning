We introduce Projection Learning, a variational framework for runtime learning in which state updates are minimal consistency repairs rather than test-time gradient steps. Given a current state and new evidence, the update is the Bregman projection onto a consistency set, or its soft proximal analogue. We prove that this operation is characterized by a Pythagorean principle: it is exactly the update that satisfies the evidence while not increasing distance to any feasible ideal state. We instantiate the framework as Conservative Projection Memory, a neural associative state whose write direction is the innovation of a key relative to the span of previously protected evidence. This yields an exact algebraic decomposition of evidence into redundant, novel, and contradictory cases. We prove feasibility and capacity laws for exact binding, conflict impossibility, hard and soft conservative projection updates, residual contraction, and ridge and Bayesian extensions. The resulting mechanism performs runtime adaptation without test-time backpropagation while making stability, plasticity, and conflict explicit mathematical objects.

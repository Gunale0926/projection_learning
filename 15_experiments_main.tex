\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:main-experiments}

The experiments are organized by claim strength.
The synthetic Bayes-gap stream tests the repair-only theorem numerically.
BalancedMask-HQS tests consequence-sensitive edge discovery with known oracle structure.
Image-HQS tests predictive usefulness under frozen image embeddings without exposing hierarchy labels.
All online learners use a prequential protocol: predict, observe the consequence, then update \citep{dawid1984prequential}.
We report the strongest completed non-DGM baselines in the main text; the appendix keeps the full tables, ablations, and the larger budget-matched baseline grid that should be completed before broader SOTA claims.
The main claims are therefore mechanism claims, not leaderboard claims.
For each experiment we state the population distinction that should matter, the learner's allowed observations, and the interpretation of the result.

\subsection{Bayes-gap calibration}

We sample
\[
Z\sim\operatorname{Unif}\{1,\ldots,K\},
\qquad
U\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2),
\]
and let the old partition observe $Z$ while the refined partition observes $(Z,U)$.
Consequences follow
\[
Y\mid Z=z,U=u
\sim
\operatorname{Bernoulli}(0.5+(-1)^{1-u}\delta).
\]
Thus the repair-only partition has risk $\log 2$ and the refined oracle has risk $H_{\rm bin}(0.5+\delta)$.
The predicted regret slope is
\[
\Delta_{\rm theory}
=
\log 2-H_{\rm bin}(0.5+\delta)
=
I(Y;U\mid Z).
\]
Figure~\ref{fig:main-evidence}A shows that the estimated repair-only slope matches this value; the $\delta=0$ control accepts no noise split.
This experiment checks the scale of the theorem rather than DGM engineering: when $U$ carries no consequence information, refinement has zero value; when $\delta$ grows, the observed repair-only regret slope grows as the conditional mutual information predicts.
The refined count learner and the one-bit DGM learner use the same information once the bit is accepted, so agreement with the oracle slope is expected.
The purpose of the plot is to show that the observed asymptotic error of repair-only prediction is the Bayes-risk gap, not a failure of a particular optimizer.

\subsection{CIFAR-100 BalancedMask-HQS}

This diagnostic isolates hierarchy edge recovery from visual recognition.
CIFAR-100 supplies fine label $z$ and coarse label $c(z)$ \citep{krizhevsky2009learning}.
Each round samples a mask $s\in\{0,1\}^{20}$ with iid $s_k\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)$ and sets
\[
Y=s_{c(z)}
\]
before flipping with probability $\eta=0.02$.
The old atom observes only $z$, so $P(Y=1\mid z)=1/2$ and $R_{\rm repair}=\log 2$.
The refined oracle observes $s_{c(z)}$, so $R_{\rm refined}=H_{\rm bin}(\eta)$.
All mask coordinates have the same marginal frequency; only the true coarse coordinate has positive conditional consequence value.
Repair-only memory remains at the $\log 2$ structure bound, frequency-only edge selection is exposed by the balanced mask marginals, and DGM-HQS recovers one fine-to-coarse edge per fine class.
Crossed logistic and a per-fine local stump also solve the task; they are included to show that the stream is learnable.
The DGM claim is structural efficiency: it reaches the oracle refined risk with the intended sparse hierarchy graph, not that no other model can fit the labels.
Budgeted cache baselines fail on the balanced-mask version because storing individual masks does not recover the reusable coordinate rule under the reported budgets.
The appendix also reports the easier single-query variant, where frequency cues are available and therefore less diagnostic.

\subsection{Non-oracle CIFAR-100 Image-HQS}

The non-oracle stream removes the fine-label atom.
The predictor and update observe a frozen image embedding $z=\phi(x)$, the mask $s$, and the binary outcome $Y=s_{c(x)}$ with the same flip probability; they never observe fine or coarse labels.
DGM uses image-only routing with a local mask-coordinate posterior memory,
\[
P(Y=1\mid j,s)
\approx
\eta+(1-2\eta)\sum_{c=1}^{20}\pi_{j,c}s_c,
\]
and updates $\pi_j$ only from the binary outcome, the mask, and the soft routing weights.
This experiment supports local posterior repair under learned visual concepts, not exact recovery of the CIFAR hierarchy graph from images.
The posterior memory is deliberately weaker than an oracle hierarchy lookup: it must infer which mask coordinates tend to matter for a routed image concept from binary outcomes alone.
The reported gain therefore tests whether local graph-memory routing provides useful specialization beyond a global frequency model and online linear heads under the same frozen encoder.
The table omits hierarchy F1 for this block because no edge-recovery claim is made in the non-oracle image setting.
Fine and coarse CIFAR labels are used only for diagnostics and representation ceilings, not in the prequential predictor or update.

In Image-HQS, paired bootstrap intervals support the NLL gaps: DGM improves over online logistic by about $0.0180$ nats with 95\% CI $[0.0148,0.0212]$, and over crossed logistic by about $0.0085$ nats with CI $[0.0047,0.0122]$.
These are limited frozen-embedding results, not a full loss-latency-memory frontier against all nonparametric and gradient baselines.
The guarded-decision and contextual XOR diagnostics are moved to Appendix~\ref{sec:experiments}; they show that the guard repairs stale local statistics when no distinction has consequence value and that local repair and edge-generated refinement are both needed on a hidden-context task.
Those diagnostics are secondary because they are synthetic, but they are important for failure analysis: without them, a positive CIFAR result could be explained by generic memory growth rather than by the refine-or-repair decision rule.

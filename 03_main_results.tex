\section{Minimal Refinement}
\label{sec:minimal-refinement}

Once a contradiction shows that the current atom is too coarse, the next question is not how to grow the largest possible model. It is: what is the least information that must be added? The primitive operation in this paper is minimal refinement. The learner has an information structure and receives evidence that a new event or variable must become observable. The correct update adds that observability and nothing else. The sigma-algebra language is standard measure-theoretic probability, while the finite partition language is the corresponding finite special case \citep{billingsley1995probability,kallenberg2002foundations}. We state the finite theorem first because it is the clearest form of the idea: split exactly the atoms crossed by the evidence.

\subsection{The finite refinement rule}

Let $\calP$ be a finite partition and let $E\subseteq\Omega$ be a new binary distinction. Define
\begin{align}
\calP_E
&=\{B\cap E:B\in\calP, B\cap E\ne\varnothing\}\notag\\
&{}\cup\{B\cap E^c:B\in\calP, B\cap E^c\ne\varnothing\}.
\label{eq:finite-refinement}
\end{align}
This notation simply keeps the nonempty pieces obtained by cutting each old atom by $E$.

\begin{theorem}[Minimal finite refinement]
\label{thm:minimal-refinement:informal}
Let $\calP$ be a finite partition of $\Omega$ and let $E\subseteq\Omega$. The partition $\calP_E$ is the unique minimal partition that refines $\calP$ and makes $E$ measurable. More precisely, if $\calQ$ refines $\calP$ and $E$ is $\calQ$-measurable, then $\calQ$ refines $\calP_E$.
\end{theorem}

The theorem gives a canonical update. We do not search over all refined representations; the evidence itself determines the least one. An atom untouched by $E$ is left unchanged. An atom crossed by $E$ is split into the two pieces that the evidence can now distinguish. This is the formal version of ``create the missing distinction and nothing else.''

\subsection{The sigma-algebra form}

For general spaces, the same operation is the join of sigma-algebras. If the current information is $\calF_t$ and the evidence is a variable $Z_t$, the minimal updated information is
\begin{align}
\calF_{t+1}=\calF_t\vee\sigma(Z_t).
\label{eq:join-update}
\end{align}

\begin{theorem}[Minimal sigma-algebra refinement]
\label{thm:sigma-refinement:informal}
Let $\calF\subseteq\calA$ be a sigma-algebra and let $Z$ be a random variable. The join $\calF\vee\sigma(Z)$ is the smallest sigma-algebra $\calG$ such that $\calF\subseteq\calG$ and $Z$ is $\calG$-measurable.
\end{theorem}

Thus the same object governs finite decision-tree splits and measurable-space refinement. A distinction is not an auxiliary feature added after learning; it is a change in the sigma-algebra of what the learner may condition on. The contradiction theorem in Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} says when such a change is necessary; minimal refinement says how little information needs to be added.

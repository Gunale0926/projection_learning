\section{Minimal Information Refinement}
\label{sec:minimal-refinement}

The primitive operation in this paper is refinement. The learner has an information structure and receives evidence that a new event or variable must become observable. The correct update should add that observability and nothing else. This section states the finite theorem first because it is the clearest form of the idea: split only the atoms cut by the evidence.

\subsection{The finite refinement rule}

Let $\calP$ be a finite partition and let $E\subseteq\Omega$ be a new binary distinction. Define
\begin{align}
\calP_E
&=\{B\cap E:B\in\calP, B\cap E\ne\varnothing\}\notag\\
&{}\cup\{B\cap E^c:B\in\calP, B\cap E^c\ne\varnothing\}.
\label{eq:finite-refinement}
\end{align}
This notation simply keeps the nonempty pieces obtained by cutting each old atom by $E$.

\begin{theorem}[Minimal finite refinement, informal version of Theorem~\ref{thm:minimal-refinement:formal}]
\label{thm:minimal-refinement:informal}
Let $\calP$ be a finite partition of $\Omega$ and let $E\subseteq\Omega$. The partition $\calP_E$ is the unique minimal partition that refines $\calP$ and makes $E$ measurable. More precisely, if $\calQ$ refines $\calP$ and $E$ is $\calQ$-measurable, then $\calQ$ refines $\calP_E$.
\end{theorem}

The theorem gives a canonical update. We do not search over all refined representations; the evidence itself determines the least one. An atom untouched by $E$ is left unchanged. An atom crossed by $E$ is split into the two pieces that the evidence can now distinguish.

\subsection{The sigma-algebra form}

For general spaces, the same operation is the join. If the current information is $\calF_t$ and the evidence is a variable $Z_t$, the minimal updated information is
\begin{align}
\calF_{t+1}=\calF_t\vee\sigma(Z_t).
\label{eq:join-update}
\end{align}

\begin{theorem}[Minimal sigma-algebra refinement, informal version of Theorem~\ref{thm:sigma-refinement:formal}]
\label{thm:sigma-refinement:informal}
Let $\calF\subseteq\calA$ be a sigma-algebra and let $Z$ be a random variable. The join $\calF\vee\sigma(Z)$ is the smallest sigma-algebra $\calG$ such that $\calF\subseteq\calG$ and $Z$ is $\calG$-measurable.
\end{theorem}

Thus the same object governs finite decision-tree splits and measurable-space refinement. A distinction is not an auxiliary feature added after learning; it is a change in the sigma-algebra of what the learner can condition on.

\subsection{Contradiction inside an atom}

Minimal refinement is necessary when the current information identifies states that the target separates. This is the simplest obstruction to pure optimization.

\begin{theorem}[Contradiction requires distinction, informal version of Theorem~\ref{thm:contradiction:formal}]
\label{thm:contradiction:informal}
Let $\calP$ be a partition of $\Omega$ and let $y:\Omega\to A$ be a required output. Suppose $\omega,\omega'$ lie in the same atom of $\calP$ but $y(\omega)\ne y(\omega')$. Then no $\calP$-measurable predictor $f:\Omega\to A$ satisfies both $f(\omega)=y(\omega)$ and $f(\omega')=y(\omega')$.
\end{theorem}

The conclusion is structural, not algorithmic. A larger step size, a different optimizer, or more iterations cannot produce two different outputs on states that the representation forces to be the same. The missing operation is not descent. It is refinement.

\section{Limitations}
\label{app:limitations}

Projection Learning is a formal update principle, not a guarantee that every source of evidence is useful. If the value map $Vh_t$ produces a poor target, projection will repair the wrong constraint. The theory also separates feasibility from generalization: exact binding says when constraints can be satisfied, but it does not imply that the resulting memory improves downstream prediction.

Conservative Projection Memory assumes that preserving old constraints is desirable. This is appropriate for stable binding, but nonstationary environments may require forgetting, reset, branching, or discounted ridge objectives. Exact innovation maintenance also has a computational cost. In large systems, one may use rank caps, approximate orthogonalization, diagonal or low-rank covariance estimates, or learned gates; these approximations are not covered by the exact preservation theorems.

The paper proves deterministic algebraic, convex-analytic, and online regression statements. It does not prove end-to-end performance improvements for language modeling, classification under shift, or long-context retrieval. Those claims require experiments. The proposed diagnostics are residual, innovation norm, conflict rate, leverage, and contraction ratio; the theory predicts their behavior under the stated model, not their empirical distribution in trained networks.

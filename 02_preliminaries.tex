\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:preliminaries}

Let $S$ be a closed convex subset of a finite-dimensional real vector space. Let $\psi:S\to\R$ be differentiable and strictly convex. The Bregman divergence generated by $\psi$ is
\begin{align*}
D_\psi(u,v)=\psi(u)-\psi(v)-\ip{\nabla\psi(v)}{u-v}.
\end{align*}
When $\psi(s)=\norm{s}_2^2/2$, this is Euclidean squared distance. All projection statements below are finite-dimensional and use the standard variational characterization of convex minimizers.

For a matrix $K$, $K^+$ denotes the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse, $\ker K$ its nullspace, and $KK^+$ the orthogonal projector onto the column span of $K$. A memory state is a matrix $W\in\R^{m\times r}$. A collection of key-value evidence is written as
\begin{align*}
K=[k_1,\ldots,k_T]\in\R^{r\times T},\qquad V=[v_1,\ldots,v_T]\in\R^{m\times T}.
\end{align*}
Exact binding means $WK=V$. A new key $k$ is decomposed relative to the old key span by
\begin{align}\label{eq:innovation}
\Pi=KK^+,\qquad z=(I-\Pi)k,\qquad a=K^+k.
\end{align}
The vector $z$ is the innovation. It is the component of $k$ not explained by previous keys.

The paper uses two levels of theorem statements. Main-text theorems are informal but mathematically precise enough to communicate the result and proof method. Each such theorem points to a formal appendix theorem. The appendix theorem links back to its main-text version.

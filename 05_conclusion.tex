\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Distinction Graph Machines turn the distinction principle into an explicit runtime learner. The model has prototypes, local memories, distinction edges, repair rules, and a train/test API. Its guiding rule is simple: refine when the concepts are wrong, repair when the local state is wrong.

The theory explains why this separation matters. Contradictions inside an atom cannot be solved by optimizing a measurable predictor. They require refinement. Once the local concept is adequate, its state can be repaired by sufficient-statistic, exemplar, ridge, or projection-memory updates. DGM turns that separation into a concrete runtime state: prototypes hold local memories, and edges record distinctions forced by conflict.

The resulting picture places gradient learning, projection repair, and distinction refinement in one hierarchy. Backpropagation moves slow parameters; projection memory repairs fast state; DGM adds the missing operation of changing the information structure on which those states are defined. In language modeling, this means that a runtime learner need not backpropagate through the entire model at inference time: it can maintain hidden-state prototypes, repair next-token memories, and create new distinctions when the stream reveals a genuinely new context.
